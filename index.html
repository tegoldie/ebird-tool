Sydney Birding Hotspots Interactive Tool

About the tool

This tool is designed for Sydney based birders to choose where and when to go to see rare species. The tool scores sites based on how often rare birds are seen there, and allows you to drill down on particular species, regions and times of year.

The tool looks at places identified on eBird as a hotspot – a popular place to go birding. Checklists submitted on eBird at these hotspots are the data source.

The data used can be inconsistent or distorted. The results should therefore be treated as a useful guide rather than a precise prediction. Access to birding hotspots can vary so make sure you google the area before you grab your binoculars and get in the car.

Scoring

Each hotspot receives a score showing how often rare birds are seen there. For example, you can expect to see 4 rare birds on a trip to a hotspot with a score of 4.

The Sydney region

This tool is intended for Sydney based birders and so looks at hotspots within a reasonable distance of Sydney.

The tool by default is set to show Greater Sydney and Illawarra. This is intended to capture areas that you could drive to in a morning from central Sydney.

You can choose the larger Greater Sydney and surrounds view which includes all the regions surrounding Sydney including the South Coast, ACT and Hunter Valley.

You can also select a specific subregion, for example Central Coast.

Months and seasons

Birds have seasonal movement patterns and knowing when to search for them is often as important as knowing where to search for them.

The tool identifies which species have seasonal patterns at which places. This is then used to calculate how each hotspot’s score changes month to month. This means you can identify when to visit the hotspot to see the most seasonal migrants.

Orange flags

The data underlying this tool is imperfect. This tool excludes hotspots with major data issues. Borderline issues with hotspot or species level data are included but with an orange flag applied to alert the user of possible issues, for example low sample size.

Source data

The tool uses the eBird Basic Dataset for New South Wales and the Australian Capital Territory. Only records associated with an eBird link are included. The analysis also uses only complete checklists—checklists where the observer indicated that all species they could identify were reported.

Checklist data can be distorted by user behaviour. For example, results may differ where the data are dominated by formal surveys, or where observers record birds heard as well as birds seen. Checklist coverage also varies between hotspots, months and years.

Records for introduced species are excluded.

Data cleaning

Records without an observation date, checklist identifier, hotspot identifier or species name are excluded.

Apparent duplicate checklists are removed. Checklists are treated as duplicates when they have matching hotspot, date, start time, protocol, duration, distance and area covered. One checklist is retained from each matching set.

Hotspot exclusions

Hotspots are excluded if:

  • They have fewer than 10 complete checklists.
  • They have no complete checklists within the last three years.
  • All complete checklists within the last three years were submitted by the same observer.
Scoring

The hotspot score estimates how many species a birder might see at a hotspot that they would not have encountered on 20 randomly selected complete checklists from across Greater Sydney and surrounds.

Regional probability

Each species is assigned a regional probability:

regional probability = regional complete checklists reporting the species ÷ all regional complete checklists

The full Greater Sydney and surrounds region is used for this calculation, regardless of the region currently selected in the report.

Rarity weighting

rarity weighting = (1 − regional probability)20

This represents the probability that the species would not appear on any of 20 randomly selected complete checklists from across Greater Sydney and surrounds.

Hotspot probability

hotspot probability = complete checklists at the hotspot reporting the species ÷ all complete checklists at the hotspot

This is a checklist-based probability. It does not account for differences in checklist duration, distance, time of day, observer experience or number of observers.

Species score contribution

species score contribution = hotspot probability × rarity weighting

A species makes a large contribution when it is both uncommon across Greater Sydney and surrounds and regularly reported at the particular hotspot.

Hotspot score

hotspot score = sum of all species score contributions

Species with a score contribution of at least 0.10 are displayed as the main species. Species with a contribution of at least 0.05 but less than 0.10 are displayed under More species. These display thresholds do not determine the hotspot score: all species contributions are included in the total.

The Sydney region

Hotspots are assigned to regions using 2021 Australian Bureau of Statistics SA4 boundaries.

Greater Sydney and Illawarra

This region includes all SA4 regions with “Sydney” in their name, Central Coast and Illawarra. This includes the Blue Mountains through the relevant Greater Sydney SA4.

Greater Sydney and surrounds

This region includes all the data in the Tool, including Greater Sydney and Illawarra, Hunter Valley, Capital Region, Central West, Southern Highlands and Shoalhaven, Newcastle and Lake Macquarie, and the Australian Capital Territory.

Jervis Bay Territory is treated as part of Southern Highlands and Shoalhaven.

Geographic limits

Hotspots west of 148.7° E or south of 36.0° S are excluded. This excludes hotspots that are west of Orange and south of Moruya, while including the Capertee Valley.

Months and seasons

Two ways of calculating monthly scores

The tool calculates monthly scores directly from a hotspot's observations when the hotspot has enough data spread across the year. For hotspots with less data, it uses seasonal patterns from the hotspot's SA4 region to estimate monthly probabilities. The regional method is described below.

Monthly scores based on hotspot observations

A hotspot has enough data for observation-based monthly scores when it has at least eight complete checklists in a month, with that level of coverage in at least nine of the twelve months. A month with fewer than eight checklists is treated as missing, not as having a probability of zero.

For hotspots that are eligible for monthly scores, the tool looks at each species’ monthly probabilities.

Raw monthly probabilities

Raw monthly probability is calculated as:

monthly probability = monthly checklists reporting the species ÷ all complete checklists at the hotspot in that month

Months here include all years, so for example February would include February 2020, February 2021 and so on.

Smoothed monthly probabilities

Raw monthly probabilities are then smoothed. For each month, the tool averages the raw monthly probability for that month with the raw monthly probabilities for the preceding and following months. This means months are weighted equally, even if there are different numbers of checklists in different months. At least two of the three monthly probabilities must be available. The calendar is circular, so December and January are treated as adjacent.

This smoothing reduces, but does not eliminate, the possibility of seasonal patterns being the result of random noise in the data.

A seasonal pattern is considered only when at least nine months have a defined smoothed probability.

Identifying the peak and trough

The tool identifies the month with the highest smoothed probability and averages the available smoothed probabilities for that month and the preceding and following months to calculate the peak three-month average. At least two of the three values must be available. It applies the same process to the month with the lowest smoothed probability to calculate the trough three-month average.

Final test for seasonality

A species is identified as seasonal if the peak is at least 1.5 times the species’ average monthly probability after smoothing, and at least 2 times the trough. When the trough is zero, the surrounding three-month trough period must contain at least 15 complete checklists to ensure the absence is not caused by a lack of observer effort.

If a species is not identified as seasonal through this test, the tool will treat it as though its probability is the same for all months.

Seasonal period

Each species identified as seasonal is given a badge with a seasonal period, for example “seasonal: Nov-Feb”.

Months with a smoothed probability of at least 60% of the highest smoothed monthly probability are potential in-season months. Starting from the peak, the tool identifies a continuous block of qualifying months.

If a month has insufficient data to determine its smoothed probability, the tool can still include that month in the seasonal period if a month meeting the test occurs immediately beyond it.

Filling missing monthly probabilities

As outlined above, some months may not have sufficient data to determine a smoothed probability. For species identified as seasonal, the tool finds the nearest month with a smoothed probability before and after the missing month and uses their average. These filled values are used for monthly display and scoring. This does not affect the test for whether a species is seasonal.

Monthly scores based on regional patterns

For a hotspot without enough data for observation-based monthly scores, the tool calculates a twelve-month probability curve for each species using complete checklists in the hotspot's SA4 region. The target hotspot is left out of this calculation, so its observations do not both define the regional pattern and provide the evidence for applying that pattern locally. Regional monthly probabilities are not smoothed because the regional sample is much larger.

The regional curve is tested for seasonality using the same peak three-month average, average monthly probability and trough three-month average described above. The additional safeguard for a zero trough is not required for regional curves because of the larger sample.

A regional seasonal curve is used only when, after leaving out the target hotspot, the species occurs on at least 0.2% of regional checklists and is reported on at least 20 regional checklists.

Checking the pattern against the hotspot

For this check, all regional months with a probability of at least 60% of the regional peak are treated as in-season; they do not need to form a continuous period. The remaining months are treated as off-season.

The check can be completed only when the hotspot has checklists in both periods and at least three in-season detections of the species. The in-season detection probability must be higher than the off-season probability. If these requirements are not met, the species keeps its raw All Year probability, is treated as non-seasonal at the hotspot and receives an “unclear seasonal patterns” orange flag.

Fitting the regional curve to the hotspot

The regional curve is adjusted to the hotspot using a single fitted value called rho. This changes the level of the probabilities while retaining the regional seasonal pattern.

hotspot monthly probability = 1 − (1 − regional monthly probability)rho

Rho is selected so that the fitted monthly probabilities, applied to the hotspot's actual checklist distribution across the year, reproduce the total number of checklists reporting the species at the hotspot.

If no value of rho can reproduce the observations, the regional curve is not applied. The species retains its raw All Year probability and receives an “unclear seasonal patterns” orange flag.

The fitted hotspot curve is then tested for seasonality again. If it no longer passes, the species is treated as non-seasonal and retains its raw All Year probability. No orange flag is applied in this case.

For accepted curves, the seasonal-period badge is calculated from the fitted hotspot curve.

All Year probability for regionally imputed species

When a species passes the regional-imputation process, its All Year probability is the equal-month average of its twelve fitted monthly probabilities. This replaces the raw checklist proportion only for that species at that hotspot. Species without an accepted imputed curve, and species at hotspots with observation-based monthly scores, retain their raw All Year probability.

Monthly scores

A score is calculated for each month using either the observation-based or regional-imputed probabilities described above.

seasonal species monthly contribution = monthly probability × rarity weighting
non-seasonal species monthly contribution = hotspot probability × rarity weighting

Only species with an identified seasonal pattern therefore cause a hotspot’s score to rise or fall across the year.

monthly hotspot score = sum of all species contributions for that month

The difference between a monthly score and the All Year score reflects the combined changes of the species identified as seasonal for that hotspot.

Orange flags

Hotspot orange flags

Hotspots are included and given an orange flag if:

  • They have 10–29 complete checklists.

Species orange flags

A species is given an orange flag for a particular hotspot if:

  • It has not been reported at the hotspot within the last three years.
  • It has been reported at the hotspot by only one observer within the last three years.
  • All its records at the hotspot fall within a maximum period of 92 days, suggesting that it may have been a one-off occurrence.
  • The regional seasonal pattern cannot be applied clearly to the hotspot. This is shown as “unclear seasonal patterns”.
